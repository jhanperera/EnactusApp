Debit Cards and Prepaid Cards

For most secondary students reading this, most banks in Canada do not allow high school students to acquire their own Credit Cards to begin with—so a debit card would be the best option to begin. But what exactly is a debit card?

Let’s begin with the obvious—a Credit Card looks awfully like a Debit Card, but do not WORK the same way. Before we have the discussion, there are different types of accounts a person may have with a bank / financial institution, however I still stick to the most basic 2 right now:

1. Checking account


This account is for day to day access to your money, however the interest rates (how much money you would get from storing money in your account) is low, if any. In addition, there may be fees for possessing such an account, depending on the type of accounts you have. For instance, most Canadian banks such as CIBC have student accounts that waive the monthly fee, so eligible students can have a checking account to access money for free. (written as of December 2016) You can access a checking account using a debit card, writing a cheque, or even setting up automatic transfers.

2. Savings Account


This account is for money that you won’t be needing, or touching, for a while. Why? A savings account gives you interest, which is basically money for putting your money with your bank. Because of this, there may be limits to the amount a person may withdrawal from the account. In addition, the savings account might even charge fees if you do not maintain a certain amount of money in the account. Once again, this account would be a great idea if you have money you won’t be needing for a while.

In short, debit cards allow access to either accounts, checking and saving, to literally take money to pay for purchases. This is great, because a person does not have to carry the exact amount required for purchases (Can you imagine how dangerous that could become?)

The FEES! OH GOSH, THE FEES!


While certain accounts have benefits and perks, such as some Student accounts with no monthly fee, this DOES NOT mean a debit card is without its fees. There are fees for Out of Network ATM fees, for using a bank machine (ATM) that is not in your bank’s network (if you used a TD machine and you’re with CIBC), debit card replacement fees, or Foreign Transactions/ ATM fees, if you make purchases outside the country or use an ATM outside of the country Canada. There are many different fees, don’t get caught surprised—read ‘em up!

While we learned previously that credit cards are spending other people’s money to finance a purchase, a debit card will use your money, in an account. This means if your account does not have enough money, you’re out of luck bud (unless you put enough in the account!) In fact, some banks charge Overdraft Fees for not having enough money in your account when using your debit card…ouch, watch out.

I will stress this again: a debit card is NOT THE SAME as a credit card. Know the difference, and make good decisions!

Finally, a prepaid card is like the cousin of Credit Cards and Debit cards, but not really. Unlike a credit card and a debit card, prepaid cards are not linked to any bank account. Instead, you are using money that has already been put into the card. The money doesn’t even have to do with the bank—there are prepaid cards with many popular stores, such a Starbucks, Walmart, EB Games…the list goes on. Once they’re empty, you can throw them out or reload, if it’s an option.

A +1 TIP


Some prepaid cards, such as Visa Prepaid Gift Cards, have fees upon activation, and THEN you load up the card with your desired amount. For instance, a $50 gift card with Visa would cost more than $50.
