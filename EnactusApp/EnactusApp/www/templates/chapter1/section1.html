What is credit, and credit rating? Why is it important?

Have you ever seen the word a dozen times, but never really thought about what exactly credit means?

In short and sweet sense, Credit, is a simple agreement, where the borrower promises to repay back the borrower at a later date. As a benefit, the borrower would be able to buy anything they want with the borrowed money, so as long as they return in the future! This is a serious advantage if you consider this: You don’t need money now to enjoy that iPhone you’ve been wanting to buy for ages! Sounds simple right?

Well, many times it isn’t just returning the original amount, or the principal say $100. Sometimes the lender will want interest payments, such as 10% of the original amount, or $10. We will get into details about this later.

person’s credit rating is basically KING in Canada. Why? A credit rating is a person’s reputation of repaying back the money owed. Think about it—would you rather lend to a friend you know who will never pay you back, or a family member that will? Credit rating tells institutions, such as banks, if a person is probable to repay back any money loaned to them.

Credit ratings, in Canada, range from R1 to R9. The lower the number, the better! The higher the number, it means the person has a poor rating, and may not be trustworthy to loan money, and expect repayment. R0 is possible, it just means the person does not have a credit rating.

Credit bureaus are organizations that gather information on borrowers. Some organizations that gather them are: Equifax Canada, TransUnion, CollectCents, etc. The history of a person’s credit rating is known as the credit history.

Got a bruise on your credit history? Fortunately, credit bureaus like Equifax only keep negative credit history information on the account for 7 years, or 10 years for business bankruptcies. The best part is, you can currently request your credit report free of charge by mail, or with a small fee online in Canada.

Finally, what exactly does the Credit rating include? You can easily remember them as the three C’s, because they measure three things about a person:
